Session Report — Retrieval Evaluation on 35 Questions & the Reranker Decision

Date: 16 July 2026 · Domain: Entrepreneurship / SME productivity · Corpus: Kibuga (5 PDFs, 47 chunks)

Locked retriever: BM25 + bge-small-en-v1.5 fused by RRF (k=60) · Repository: github.com/notomodo/adtc-2026

This session did three things: expanded the retrieval test set from 19 to 35 questions to guard against overfitting; ran the locked retriever against the larger set to settle the k=3 chunk-count decision; and ran a controlled cross-encoder reranker experiment to decide whether to reopen the deferred reranker choice. The headline outcome is a genuine engineering trade-off with no free lunch: the reranker measurably improves retrieval quality but at a latency cost that may be disqualifying on the target hardware.

  • The test-set expansion worked as intended. Enlarging from 19 to 35 questions dropped headline R@5 from 89% to 83% — not a regression, but the removal of an optimistic bias. The original 19 questions probed only 9 of 47 chunks, mostly the easy returns/support clauses. The truer number is 83%.
  • k=3 is confirmed defensible on the larger set. Choosing to pass 3 chunks instead of 5 into the LLM costs exactly one question (Q17, a prose question at rank 4). None of the 16 new questions fall in the k=3 loss band.
  • The reranker helps — but only at the smallest candidate window, and at a steep latency price. At N=10 it lifts R@5 to 89% and MRR to 0.783; widening the window buys nothing and regresses more questions. The cost is ~2.4 seconds of added CPU latency per query, versus ~86 ms for retrieval alone.
  • The deepest weakness is structural, not fixable by reranking. Three legal-inference questions cannot be rescued by either retrieval stage, because both the embedder and the reranker are small general-purpose models that do not encode legal semantics. This is a documented competence boundary, not a bug.

2. Test-set expansion (overfitting guard)

The prior evaluation used 19 auto-generated questions. Because those questions shared a generation distribution and concentrated on a handful of chunks, there was a real risk that retrieval quality was being measured on cases the system was always going to pass. We added 16 new questions, each grounded by reading the actual corpus text, deliberately over-weighting the two hard strata (prose-inference and multi-chunk) and spreading across previously untested chunks and the under-tested Sellers-Terms and Privacy-Policy documents.

Why the number went down, and why that's good The original 19 questions reproduce their previous ranks exactly, so every point of the R@5 drop (89% → 83%) comes from the 16 new questions surfacing failures the narrow set was hiding. A technical panel that stress-tests the question set will now find it already stressed.

3. Baseline retrieval on 35 questions

The locked hybrid was run unmodified against the 35-question set. Provenance was verified: the committed retriever module was imported (not reimplemented), the run was offline and on CPU, a SHA-256 corpus fingerprint gate passed, and the 19 overlapping questions reproduced the previous run to the digit — confirming an identical, deterministic pipeline.

The k=3 decision

Passing fewer chunks into the LLM means a smaller, faster prompt and less "lost in the middle" dilution, at the risk of dropping the answer. The recall curve knees sharply at k=3 (R@1 60%, R@3 80%, R@5 83%, R@10 91%): the 1→3 gain is 20 points, 3→5 buys only 3. Reading the full rank vector, dropping from k=5 to k=3 costs exactly one question — Q17, an original prose question at rank 4. Six questions miss even at k=5, so k has no effect on them. k=3 is confirmed on 35 questions, not just 19.

4. Reranker experiment

A cross-encoder reranker (cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L6-v2, ~80 MB) was added as a new stage after the locked hybrid. For each question it re-scores the hybrid's top-N candidates by reading query and chunk together, then re-sorts them. The candidate window N was swept over {10, 15, 20}. The hybrid itself was not modified. Results are deterministic (identical across four runs); latency is a noisy single-box CPU measurement.

Figure 3 — Recall and MRR across candidate windows. All three windows reach the same recall; widening N past 10 adds only latency and regressions.

The counterintuitive core finding Widening the candidate window does not help — it hurts. The reachability ceiling predicted that N=20 could reach all six baseline misses. It reached them and then scored the three hardest legal-inference chunks below their distractors, actively demoting them (Q19 12→19, Q35 13→17, Q29 stuck at 20). Reaching a chunk is not the same as ranking it correctly; here, reaching backfired. The reranker's entire usefulness lives in the top-10.

Latency: the crux of the decision

Figure 4 — Per-query latency (log scale). Retrieval is ~86 ms; the reranker adds ~2.4 s at N=10 — roughly a 28× increase — measured on the pessimistic i5-4300U floor.

This is the decision. The reranker buys +6pp R@5 and +0.079 MRR, but converts a sub-100 ms query into a multi-second one. On the deployment reference machine (8 GB, 10th–12th-gen i5) the absolute latency will be lower than on the i5-4300U test floor, but the ~28× ratio is a property of the model, not the box. For an interactive SME assistant, a 1–2.5 second wait per question before the LLM even begins generating is a material usability cost.

5. Per-question effects: rescues and regressions

Aggregate recall hides the per-question picture. At the best window (N=10) the reranker improved 7 questions, worsened 2, and left 26 unchanged — a net of +5. The regression audit is essential: a reranker that fixes prose while breaking a working question is a bad trade.

The regression that matters Q31 was a perfect rank-1 answer and the reranker demoted it to rank 4 — out of the k=3 window. This dropped near_miss R@3 from 100% to 75%. It is the concrete instance of the reranker trap: improving the hard cases can silently break easy ones. Any ship decision must weigh this, not just the aggregate gain.

6. The structural ceiling on prose

Three legal-inference questions resist every lever tried: Q19 (account suspension without notice), Q35 (which courts hold jurisdiction), and Q29 (right to data erasure). Their gold chunks sit deep in the ranking (12, 13, 20), and when the cross-encoder is finally shown them, it scores them below the distractors. The reason is the same for both retrieval stages: the bi-encoder (bge-small, 33M params) and the cross-encoder (MiniLM, MS-Marco-trained) are both small, general-purpose models. Neither was trained to recognize that "whose courts decide our dispute" means the same as a clause headed "Law and jurisdiction." This is a competence boundary of small models, not a defect in the architecture.

What this means for the design narrative Retrieval is strong across factual and paraphrase queries; the known weak spot is abstract legal-inference phrasing. It is documented, measured, and bounded. Owning a measured limitation — rather than hiding it behind an averaged number — is the kind of rigor a technical panel rewards. The fix (query expansion for legal vocabulary, or a domain-aware embedder) is a v2 research direction, not a v1 requirement.

7. Reproducibility & provenance

  • Committed pipeline reused, not reimplemented — the retriever module was imported directly (MD5-matched to the repository tarball).
  • Offline & CPU-only — both the embedder and the reranker loaded from local cache with the model hub disabled; no network access.
  • Deterministic — ranks and scores reproduced identically across four runs; a SHA-256 corpus fingerprint gate passed before every run.
  • v2 cross-check passed — the 19 overlapping questions reproduced the previous run exactly, proving the pipeline is unchanged.
  • Environment deviation logged — the run used sentence-transformers 5.6.0 (repo pin is 3.3.1); the embedding math is identical across these versions and the exact v2 reproduction confirms ranks are unaffected. For an auditable "pinned stack produces X" claim, one confirming run from a pinned virtualenv is recommended.
  • Labels — the 16 new gold labels remain single-annotator and unverified against the source PDFs by design; the multi-chunk labels in particular warrant a human spot-check before being treated as final gold.

8. Decisions & open items

Confirmed this session

  • Test set expanded to 35 questions with 49% corpus coverage; hard strata over-weighted.
  • k=3 chunk count confirmed defensible on the larger set (costs one prose question).
  • Prose (legal-inference) identified and measured as the single localized retrieval weakness.

Open — requires a human call

  • Reranker ship / no-ship. The trade is +6pp R@5 and +0.079 MRR against ~2.4 s/query added latency and one meaningful regression (Q31). This report presents the evidence; it does not make the call. Recommended framing: is a multi-second per-query wait acceptable for the SME use case, and can the Q31-type regression be tolerated or mitigated?
  • Generation layer is still entirely unmeasured. Every result here concerns retrieval — whether the correct chunk is found. Whether the LLM correctly uses a retrieved chunk to answer is untested, and it is what the human panel actually experiences. This is now the largest unquantified risk in the system.
  • Hand-labelled held-out set from a person who did not see the corpus — the remaining fix for query-distribution bias.
  • Zero measurements on the deployment reference machine — all latency figures are from the pessimistic i5-4300U floor.

Recommendation on the reranker

Do not ship the reranker as an always-on stage on the strength of these numbers alone. The latency cost is severe on the target hardware and it introduces a regression on a previously correct answer. A defensible middle path, if the recall gain is wanted: apply the reranker selectively — only when the hybrid's top scores are close or low-confidence — so most queries pay no latency and only ambiguous ones incur the cost. That is a new experiment, not a conclusion of this one. The higher-priority next step is measuring the generation layer, which gates the score the panel gives you far more directly than the last 6 points of retrieval recall.
